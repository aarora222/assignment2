# White Hat Write-Up 

This visualization performs analysis on data from the World Bank's "World Development Indicators" dataset. It includes categorical variables for geographic region like "Country", socioeconomic classifications like "IncomeGroup", and quantitative variables to track yearly changes for gender equality indicators across different industries (e.g., the amount of females employed in agriculture). 

We set both sets of female and male bars at a central x-value, utilizing a scale to calculate positive widths for male bars extending to the right, while calculating negative coordinate offsets and widths for the female bars extending leftwards. This presents viewers with a diverging bar chart, making comparisons between neighboring countries and gender gaps within those countries easily identifiable. We colored male and female data in steelblue and hot pink respectively. These colors provide a modern aesthetic that allows for easy distinction between the two groups. 

This visualization includes interactive filtering made possible by attaching an event listener to two dropdown menu options that update employment and income global variables (respectively), triggering the visualization to be redrawn. This allows viewers to make comparisons and draw their own conclusions about gender equality by referencing a multitude of different indicators.

In addition to this, we also used a d3-simple-slider to allow users to manually drag and change the global targetYear variable to a value between 1991 and 2019. There are 1 second transitions (implemented with the D3 transition interface) called whenever the visualization is updated - specifically when the dropdown menus or slider is used. This allows viewers to track how individual countries shift without experiencing a jarring replacement of data. This updates the visualization to reflect data for the year in reference. Perhaps the most important part of an effective visualization is providing a wide range of information that is easily digestible, making these features important.

Math.abs is used to calculate how far male-to-female ratios are from 1 for user-chosen indicators. This is implemented into a sorting algorithm, which places countries with the strongest calculated gender equality (closest ratio to 1) at the top and countries with the weakest calculated gender equality at the bottom (furthest ratio from 1). There is a line of text mentioning that the data is sorted in this manner under the title, providing viewers with full context before looking at the visualization. This allows users to draw comparisons between and amongst countries across different indicators very simply and to observe information in a more visually appealing, properly sequenced manner. 

A box plot is also displayed alongside the main chart, summarizing the distribution of male and female employment shares across all displayed countries. It is generated by computing quartile stats from filtered data with the d3.quantile functions and rendered with a box, median line, and whisker element for each gender group. Like the rest of the visualization, it transitions smoothly whenever filters are updated, ensuring it always reflects the currently displayed data. This provides viewers with information about the spread and distribution of both gender groups, allowing them to draw stronger conclusions about the existence of inequalities. 


# Black Hat Write-Up

This visualization performs a (deliberately) selective analysis on data from the World Bank's "World Development Indicators" dataset. This visualization restricts the income group dropdown to only three options - High, Upper middle, and Lower middle income - by adjusting a localVariable to only include parts of an array of incomeLevels instead of all of the array. Excluding low income and all income groups limits the data to support the idea that the worker gap is solved when it is not. Additionally, the employment type is hardcoded to 'Wage and salaried workers' by assigning a non-functional string to the CSS selector variable. This prevents a dropdown from rendering, locking the visualization to a single employment category. We deliberately provide the user with less information to draw conclusions about gender inequalities under specific employment types. For example, "Employment in Industry" is excluded, which includes a much higher employment share of men than women. 

This visualization also sorts by placing countries with ratios of male to female that are most similar towards the top and countries with the largest gender gap at the bottom. However, unlike the first visualization, there is no textual mention that this is how the data is sorted. This creates an impression that employment measures are fairly equal between men and women, since the most equal countries are more prominently placed at the top of our visualization. A viewer that does not scroll past the initial view may not realize that the gender gap is larger than seen at first glance. 

The second visualization also uses a title (World Bank Data Suggest Worker Gap Nearly Solved in Middle and High-Income Nations) designed to push viewers towards the conclusion that the worker gap is solved in middle and high-income nations. This title is not necessarily false, but utilizing it without mentioning the disparities in lower-income nations or specific types of employment provides viewers with an incomplete picture. A more neutral title would acknowledge the limitations of the data being shown.

Outside of these changes, the black hat visualization includes a lot of the same features as the white hat visualization. Namely, it also has a box-plot alongside the main chart, a slider to change the specific year of data that the visualization renders, and a diverging bar chart with steelblue and hot pink bars to represent the male and female groups respectively. These components make the visualization more engaging, which allows viewers to absorb information more easily. In an unbiased visualization, these features are invaluable tools for exploration and understanding. In a biased one, however, they can lend an air of credibility and interactivity that distracts viewers from the selective choices made in the underlying data and design.
