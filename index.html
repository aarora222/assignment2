Gender Employment Share by Country, Income Group, and Year

This is an interactive view of male vs. female employment shares by country and year, shown for selected employment types and income groups derived from data collected by The World Bank, an organization that plays a vital role in collecting internationally comparable statistics about global development. Countries are ordered by most even to most lopsided gender split. Additionally box-plots display the overall distribution shares for male and female employed populations from the filters selected.

The types of employment are as follows:

  • Wage and salaried workers: People who work for an employer and are paid wages or salaries.
  • Employment in services: Workers employed in service-sector jobs like retail, education, healthcare, finance, and public services.
  • Employment in industry: Workers employed in industry, including manufacturing, construction, mining, and utilities.
  • Contributing family workers: People who work in a family business or farm without formal pay.
  • Vulnerable employment: Workers in less secure jobs, typically own-account workers and contributing family workers.
  • Self-employed: People who work for themselves rather than for an employer.
  • Employers: Self-employed people who run a business and hire others.
  • Employment in agriculture: Workers employed in farming, forestry, fishing, and related agricultural activity.

It is important to note that types of employment are not mutually exclusive.

White Hat Write-Up

This visualization performs analysis on data from the World Bank's "World Development Indicators" dataset. It includes categorical variables for geographic region, like "Country", socioeconomic classifications like "IncomeGroup", and quantitative variables to track yearly changes for gender equality indicators across different industries (e.g., the number of women employed in agriculture).

We set both sets of female and male bars at a central x-value, utilizing a scale to calculate positive widths for male bars extending to the right, while calculating negative coordinate offsets and widths for the female bars extending leftwards. This presents viewers with a diverging bar chart, making comparisons between neighboring countries and gender gaps within those countries easily identifiable. We colored male and female data in steelblue and hot pink, respectively. These colors provide a modern aesthetic that allows for easy distinction between the two groups.

This visualization includes interactive filtering made possible by attaching an event listener to two dropdown menu options that update employment and income global variables (respectively), triggering the visualization to be redrawn. This allows viewers to make comparisons and draw their own conclusions about gender equality by referencing a multitude of different indicators.

In addition to this, we also used a d3-simple-slider to allow users to manually drag and change the global targetYear variable to a value between 1991 and 2019. There are 1-second transitions (implemented with the D3 transition interface) called whenever the visualization is updated — specifically when the dropdown menus or slider is used. This allows viewers to track how individual countries shift without experiencing a jarring replacement of data. Perhaps the most important part of an effective visualization is providing a wide range of information that is easily digestible, making these features important.

Math.abs is used to calculate how far male-to-female ratios are from 1 for user-chosen indicators. This is implemented into a sorting algorithm which places countries with the strongest calculated gender equality (closest ratio to 1) at the top, and countries with the weakest at the bottom. There is a line of text mentioning that the data is sorted in this manner under the title, providing viewers with full context before looking at the visualization.

A box plot is also displayed alongside the main chart, summarizing the distribution of male and female employment shares across all displayed countries. It is generated by computing quartile statistics from the filtered data using d3.quantile, and rendered with a box, median line, and whisker element for each gender group. Like the rest of the visualization, it transitions smoothly whenever filters are updated, ensuring it always reflects the currently displayed data. This provides viewers with information about the spread and distribution of both gender groups, allowing them to draw stronger conclusions about the existence of inequalities.

Black Hat Write-Up

This visualization performs a deliberately selective analysis on data from the World Bank's "World Development Indicators" dataset. The income group dropdown is restricted to only three options — High, Upper middle, and Lower middle income — by slicing the incomeLevels array to exclude "All income" and "Low income" classifications. Excluding low income groups limits the data to support the idea that the worker gap is solved when it is not. Additionally, the employment type is hardcoded to 'Wage and salaried workers' by assigning a non-functional string to the CSS selector variable, preventing the dropdown from functioning. This deliberately provides the user with less information to draw conclusions about gender inequalities under specific employment types. For example, "Employment in Industry" is excluded, which includes a much higher employment share of men than women.

The second visualization also uses a title (World Bank Data Suggest Worker Gap Nearly Solved in Middle and High-Income Nations) designed to push viewers towards the conclusion that the worker gap is solved in middle and high-income nations. This title is not necessarily false, but utilizing it without mentioning the disparities in lower-income nations or specific types of employment provides viewers with an incomplete picture. A more neutral title would acknowledge the limitations of the data being shown. Additionally, the x-axis does not specify what the employment share is based from (whether the share is out of all the employed males/females, or out of the entire male/female population), which adds an additional layer deception, as computing from employed populations would lead to a large difference than computing from whole populations for countries where the rate of employment may already be low.

Outside of these changes, the black hat visualization includes many of the same features as the white hat visualization — a box plot, a caption, a slider to change the year, and a diverging bar chart with steelblue and hot pink bars. These components make the visualization more engaging, allowing viewers to absorb information more easily. In an unbiased visualization, these features are invaluable tools for exploration and understanding. In a biased one, however, they lend an air of credibility and interactivity that distracts viewers from the selective choices made in the underlying data and design.
